Regime
The dominant story today is geopolitical, not monetary. Iran and the US have re-escalated directly: an Iranian strike on tankers in Hormuz was met with US airstrikes on Iranian targets, and the timing lines up with the funeral of a former Iranian supreme leader yesterday, which drew large crowds and leaves the door open to further hostilities. Talks between the two sides on a Hormuz transit mechanism, let alone the more sensitive nuclear file, had made no real progress even before this latest exchange, and most of the recent escalatory moves have come from the Iranian side amid an internal power struggle between hardliners and moderates. Oil is the clean read on credibility here: spot WTI is back above $73, essentially erasing the prior two weeks of decline in a single move.
Central bank stances
Fed
Hold at 3.50–3.75%. Wednesday's Minutes were not dovish — several members reportedly wanted to hike immediately at the June meeting, and while the majority preferred to wait for more data, that option stays fully open. Inflation dominated the discussion: tariff effects, the earlier Middle East-driven energy spike, and a new theme — the AI capex cycle's near-term demand pull on data centers, chips, and power raising inflation risk even as it may prove disinflationary longer-run through productivity. Market still prices roughly 80% odds of a 25bp hike by September, with a further hike close to fully priced by March 2027. Warsh testifies before Congress next week.
ECB
Steady at 2.25% deposit rate. The RBNZ's hawkish hike (below) is being read by some as a signal that other hawkish-leaning central banks, ECB included, might look past the recent oil pullback and consider tightening again as soon as the next meeting to guard against second-round inflation effects — worth watching as a genuine cross-CB read-through, not just a domestic NZD story.
BoJ
Structurally passive despite the hike already delivered. Markets are almost fully pricing one more 25bp hike by year-end, yet continue selling yen anyway on PM Takaichi's low-rate advocacy and her influence over Board appointments — one of her appointees already voted against the June hike, another leans dovish, and two hawkish members' terms expire next summer, opening the door to a more dovish Board over time regardless of near-term hikes.
RBNZ
Confirmed 25bp hike to OCR 2.50%, as flagged Wednesday. Explicit reasoning: limiting cost pass-through to consumer prices and avoiding unwarranted further easing of financial conditions, with the Iran-driven oil move likely a live input to the decision. The Governor left the door open to further hikes, and noted 2.50% still sits at the accommodative end of the estimated 2.5%–3.5% neutral range — this is not a one-and-done move.
BoE / RBA / SNB / BoC
BoE steady, sterling still outperforming into the July 20 PM handover. RBA neutral-to-hawkish undertone, unchanged. SNB unchanged. BoC neutral heading into today's employment print, the week's most consequential data point for CAD.
Oil — the mechanism behind the floor
Even though headline Hormuz traffic has partially recovered, that recovery is misleading: shipping insurers are charging steep premiums for any vessel entering or leaving the Gulf, and very few new tankers are entering to load fresh cargo even though ships have been exiting. Every new tanker that does enter will carry both that insurance premium and Iranian transit fees, which feed directly into the price of oil. As long as the situation stays this fragile, a drop much below $60–65 looks unlikely purely on structural risk premium, regardless of headline de-escalation. A fresh full Hormuz closure, collapsed talks, or new infrastructure strikes would send oil back toward $100 without much resistance. Today's move is a live example of that asymmetry playing out.
