Regime
A genuinely volatile 48 hours. Monday's dollar strength, driven by the weekend's oil spike reviving Fed hike bets, fully reversed by Tuesday: hawkish comments from a Fed governor (rates could rise further if inflation stays well above target) briefly pushed September hike pricing to fully-priced-and-then-some and July meeting odds to 43%, before Tuesday's CPI showed core inflation cooling for the first time this cycle — the second consecutive month of genuinely cooler data after the earlier NFP miss. The dollar gave back essentially all of Monday's gains. The desk's own proprietary read stays deliberately cautious on chasing that dollar weakness with fresh shorts: two live risks could reverse it just as fast — a hawkish surprise from Warsh's Senate testimony today, or the reheating Iran conflict keeping an oil-driven inflation floor under the whole picture regardless of what core CPI just showed. The plan flagged is to wait for Thursday's Retail Sales before adding fresh dollar-short exposure, using a soft print there as confirmation that the CPI cooling reflects genuine consumer weakness rather than just an energy base effect.
Central bank stances
Fed
Warsh testifies before the Senate Banking Committee today, having already appeared before the House Financial Services Committee yesterday. Today's PPI print lands ahead of that testimony. A hawkish tone today — "the Fed won't tolerate persistently elevated inflation" — would revive the hike odds that just retreated on CPI; a dovish hint would validate this week's dollar weakness properly for the first time.
ECB
Steady, no fresh signal since last week's hold at 2.25% deposit rate. Money markets in some reads are pricing a September hike, though this isn't corroborated as strongly as the Fed-side story this week.
BoJ
Reports suggest the BoJ is considering raising its 2026 growth forecast while continuing to flag inflation risk — on paper, a hawkish-leaning update. The yen's own price action doesn't reflect it: Friday's GPIF-driven strength has already faded this week, consistent with the market continuing to treat yen dips as dollar-buying opportunities rather than pricing a genuinely more restrictive BoJ, especially given PM Takaichi's continued preference for dovish-leaning Board appointees.
BoC
Decision today. Consensus across surviving sources expects a hold at 2.25%. The real question isn't the rate itself but the accompanying language — whether the statement explicitly closes the door on further easing (the exact condition of the USD/CAD Thesis Invalidation) or leaves it open. This is a live, same-day test.
RBNZ / RBA / BoE / SNB
No fresh signals from any of the four today. NZD continues to be shaped more by broader risk-flow dynamics this week than by anything domestic.
Geopolitical
Both the US and Iran continue signaling they want to avoid a full regional war while trading limited, targeted strikes — a pattern of mutual deterrence rather than open conflict, but a genuinely unstable one. Weekend US strikes hit Iranian missile systems, drone infrastructure, and facilities Washington characterizes as threats to shipping; Iran retaliated against US regional interests and reiterated it could further restrict Hormuz traffic if it judges its security interests threatened. Traffic through the strait has fallen to minimal levels despite not being fully closed. Trump has reimposed a blockade on Iranian oil shipments and added a 20% toll for vessels transiting under US naval escort. Qatari and Omani mediation made no real progress over the weekend; rhetoric has hardened on both sides, with the focus shifting from diplomacy toward military signaling. Whether this settles back into limited tit-for-tat strikes or escalates further is the single biggest unknown hanging over the rest of this week.
