Forward Guidance Gone
!
This week marked a genuine regime change in how the Fed operates, not just a rate decision. Warsh's debut press conference eliminated forward guidance entirely, removed the Chair's own dot, and launched five task forces to rebuild the Fed's communication and operational framework from first principles. The market's read — "hawkish despite the hold" — combined with the Iran MOU formal signing today, creates the most information-dense week since the conflict began three months ago.
FOMC — The Real Story Behind the Hold
What Changed Structurally
FOMC held 12-0 at 3.50-3.75%, but the actual news was structural, not the rate. Warsh eliminated forward guidance from the statement — he stated markets operate more efficiently reacting to incoming data than guessing the Fed's next move. The policy statement was cut to roughly 150 words, a third of its prior length, deliberately stripped of "central-bank language." Warsh did not submit his own dot in the Summary of Economic Projections — a explicit signal he does not want projections treated as promises. He described colleagues' dots as submitted "with pencils... with big erasers," revealing a genuinely fractured committee: the SCP showed a 50/50 split between members wanting rates flat-to-lower versus higher by year-end. Five task forces launched covering communications, balance sheet policy, data sources, AI/productivity, and the inflation framework itself — findings due by end of 2026. Warsh affirmed the 2% target is "unambiguously and unanimously" non-negotiable until achieved.
Why Markets Read This as Hawkish
Despite the unanimous hold, multiple sources confirm the market reaction was unambiguously hawkish: "Dollar jumps as Fed holds rates but projects one hike later this year," "Dollar hits one-year high on Fed hike bets," "Dollar clings to two-month peak as Fed rate-hike bets mount." The SCP median showed 9 of 12 members expecting at least one further 25bp hike by year-end. S&P 500 fell 1.2% post-decision; bond yields rose as investors interpreted the elimination of forward guidance and the inflation framing ("inflation is a choice") as signalling the Fed will not pre-commit to easing even with Iran de-escalation reducing energy-driven price pressure. The death of forward guidance mechanically raises volatility — without the Fed smoothing expectations ahead of data, reactions to CPI, NFP, and retail sales should now be sharper and more violent than in the pre-Warsh regime. This is a structural change to how every future Tuesday/Wednesday data release will trade, not a one-week event.
The Other Three Central Bank Decisions This Week
SNB (Thursday): Held at 0.00%. Inflation forecast revised up to 0.6% for 2026 (from 0.5%), 0.6% for 2027, 0.7% for 2028 — still firmly within price stability range. SNB raised willingness to intervene against excessive CHF appreciation, noting the franc has actually depreciated somewhat as global rate differentials widened. Switzerland flagged as economically resilient despite Middle East disruption — 2026 growth estimate ~1%, 2027 ~1.5%.

BoE (Thursday): Held at 3.75%. Governor Bailey's pooled broadcast interview post-decision revealed genuine three-part caution despite welcoming the Iran de-escalation: (1) need to see the peace actually hold and settle, (2) need an assessment of how much damage was done to energy supply infrastructure — "probably more of an issue with gas than oil," (3) need to see whether the inflation overshoot (higher than BoE expected) shows persistence given the UK's softened economic backdrop. Bailey explicitly stated he expected the UK would be back at the 2% target by now and is not yet there.

RBA (earlier in week, official statement reviewed): Held at 4.35% unanimously. The actual official statement is more nuanced than midweek's brief credited — it explicitly retains hawkish optionality: "it will do what it considers necessary... including increasing the cash rate target further if required." Statement flags continued capacity pressure inflation risk and explicitly notes plausible scenarios where inflation runs higher and activity lower than the May baseline given the still-early-stage Middle East resolution.

Iran MOU — Today Is the Formal Signing
Iran has published a fully signed Memorandum of Understanding, which it states is already in effect. Formal ceremony proceeds today at Bürgenstock, Switzerland. The full text (Iranian-published version) confirms: immediate and permanent ceasefire across all fronts including Lebanon; mutual sovereignty respect; 60-day deadline for a final agreement (extendable by mutual consent); immediate naval blockade removal start, full removal within 30 days; Hormuz safe passage for commercial vessels with zero fees for the first 60 days, technical/military obstacles and demining within 30 days; a minimum $300bn Iran reconstruction fund (financing mechanism to be defined in the final agreement); full sanctions removal (UN Security Council, IAEA Board, US executive and primary/secondary sanctions) with a detailed schedule deferred to the final agreement; Iran confirms no nuclear weapons development, enriched uranium stockpile resolution under IAEA-supervised mechanism, continued negotiation on future enrichment rights; status quo maintained during negotiations (no new sanctions, no additional US force deployment); immediate licensing for Iranian oil/petrochemical exports and associated banking/insurance/transport services; unfreezing of restricted Iranian assets with licenses for use via Iran's central bank; a joint monitoring mechanism; final agreement requires binding UN Security Council resolution.

Domestic political friction: Trump allies are publicly criticizing the emerging deal over sanctions relief scope, Iranian asset access, and the $300bn fund size — this is a genuine domestic ratification risk that did not exist in this form last week.

Dominant Theme
This week delivered the most structurally significant Fed event in years, and it had nothing to do with the rate decision itself. Warsh eliminated forward guidance, refused to submit his own dot, and launched a five-task-force review of how the Fed operates from first principles. The market's read — hawkish despite the hold, dollar to one-year highs — combined with a genuinely fractured 50/50 committee, tells you this is now a data-reactive Fed rather than a guidance-driven one. Every future CPI, NFP, and retail sales print should be expected to move markets harder than it did under the old regime. Layered on top: the Iran MOU's formal signing today, with new domestic US political friction over deal terms that did not exist a week ago. The two BoJ and RBA primary statements reviewed this week revealed genuinely hawkish/balanced central bank language that was undersold in earlier mid-week characterization — corrections are noted explicitly above rather than quietly carried forward.
Primary Risk — Volatility Regime Cuts Both Ways
The death of forward guidance means sharper reactions to data in both directions. A soft Core PCE print next Friday, in this new regime, could reverse the dollar's one-year-high move just as violently as the FOMC hawkish read built it. Position sizing needs to account for genuinely higher realized volatility on every release going forward — this is not a one-week phenomenon, it is the new baseline.
Secondary Risk — Iran Deal Domestic Ratification
Trump allies publicly criticizing the deal's sanctions relief scope, asset access, and fund size is a new and real risk to the 60-day final-agreement timeline. If domestic political pressure forces a renegotiation or walk-back of terms, expect oil to spike back and the broad de-escalation trade to reverse sharply — this would affect the AUD/USD short (removing risk-on AUD support, paradoxically helping the short) and gold (geopolitical premium returns) most directly.
