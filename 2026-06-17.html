What Happened Monday-Tuesday — The Twin Resolution
BoJ Delivered Tuesday — As Expected, Not as Hoped
BoJ raised the policy rate from 0.75% to 1.0% — the highest level since 1995, first hike since December, 7-1 vote (Ueda absent for medical treatment; Asada the lone dissenter citing growth risk over inflation risk). The hike was fully priced — JPY did NOT strengthen materially because markets had positioned for a possible 50bp surprise that did not materialize. BoJ flagged accelerating cost pass-through risk and signalled continued gradual hikes roughly every six to twelve months. Wholesale inflation hit 6.3% in May — a three-year high — confirming the energy-cost pass-through thesis. Critical nuance: despite the hike, JPY remains structurally weak because inflation stays below the 2% target even as global yields rise elsewhere — this is JPY-negative even with BoJ tightening.
RBA Held Tuesday — Hawkish Hold That Wasn't Hawkish Enough
RBA held as expected following weaker domestic inflation, consumption, and employment data. The Iran peace deal and Hormuz reopening reduced inflation risk premium in the announcement language. AUD reacted NEGATIVELY because the statement lacked sufficiently hawkish components. Swap markets now price only ~30% probability of an August hike and just 16bp of total additional tightening by year-end — effectively less than one more hike. RBA has hiked 75bp total since February. Q1 GDP grew just 0.3% q/q as households cut spending; unemployment rose to 4.5% — a four-and-a-half-year high. The combination of weak growth and a less-hawkish-than-expected statement is AUD-negative independent of the Iran deal dynamic.
The Iran MOU — From Rumor to Signed Document
Critical escalation in confirmation level: Trump, Vance, and Iran's Ghalibaf have electronically signed the US-Iran peace MOU. Formal ceremony scheduled for Friday June 19 at the Bürgenstock resort in Switzerland — confirmed by the Swiss Foreign Ministry. Trump stated the Strait of Hormuz will be "fully open by Friday." This is materially more concrete than Sunday's Pakistan-mediated "deal text" announcement — this is an actual signed document with a confirmed venue and date.

Per the Iranian-side draft (US has not officially confirmed all points): full lifting of the naval blockade, suspension of Iranian oil sanctions, permanent ceasefire including Lebanon, Hormuz reopening within 30 days, a $300+ billion reconstruction plan, 60 days of further nuclear negotiations excluding missile programs and resistance groups, release of $24 billion in frozen Iranian funds (half released before final-agreement talks begin), and UN Security Council ratification of any final deal.

Structural analysis flags two tiers of confirmation: well-confirmed (blockade lift, Hormuz opening, ceasefire including Lebanon, 60-day nuclear talks, temporary sanctions relief) versus US-unconfirmed (the $300bn fund, missile program exclusion, the $24bn release terms, UN ratification requirement). The structural read: by accepting this memorandum, Iran has effectively "won" — frozen assets released, sanctions eased, and Hormuz control retained. The US achieved none of its original objectives and exits as the strategic loser. The memorandum barely addresses the nuclear question that started the conflict, and Iranian officials' statements suggest enriched uranium will not leave the country. Markets should expect this ceasefire to last through summer and the US midterm cycle to calm gasoline prices, with renewed escalation risk after the season — this is the working base case, not confirmed fact.

The Signed MOU — Confirmed Details
Trump, Vance, and Iran's Ghalibaf have electronically signed the peace memorandum. Formal ceremony confirmed by the Swiss Foreign Ministry for Friday June 19 at the Bürgenstock resort, central Switzerland. Trump publicly stated Hormuz will be "fully open by Friday." Structural Caution — Strategic Assessment

Base case working assumption: any ceasefire likely holds through summer and the US midterm cycle (easing gasoline price pressure) with renewed escalation risk after the season — this is analytical judgment, not confirmed fact. The US position (Trump: "toll-free opening") and the Iranian position (continued Iranian administration with potential fees) remain publicly different interpretations of the same agreement — a real source of forward uncertainty.

Dominant Theme
Two of the week's three central bank catalysts have resolved without surprise — BoJ hiked as expected without lifting JPY, RBA held without sufficient hawkishness to support AUD. The dominant market driver this week has shifted from "central bank divergence" to "Iran de-escalation magnitude" — the electronically signed MOU and Trump's Hormuz-open-by-Friday statement are doing more to move FX and oil than the rate decisions themselves. This is exactly the dynamic flagged in last weekend's analysis: geopolitical headlines are more important than the data right now, because the data itself (inflation prints, oil-driven CPI/PPI) has been a downstream consequence of the conflict for three months. FOMC tonight is the remaining swing factor, and the setup is genuinely two-sided: oil collapse gives Warsh room to sound less hawkish than markets feared, which would be a USD headwind layered on top of the Iran-deal USD weakness already in play.
Primary Risk — Sustained Iran De-escalation Becomes the New Regime
If the Friday signing proceeds smoothly, Hormuz genuinely reopens, and oil stays depressed, the entire macro narrative of the past three months (energy-driven inflation forcing hawkish central banks) unwinds. This would be USD-negative independent of FOMC tonight, AUD-supportive on improved risk sentiment (working against Setup 2), and CAD-negative on weak oil (working against Setup 1's bullish thesis via a different channel than originally anticipated). This is not a Thesis Invalidation for any named setup, but it is a regime-level risk that deserves explicit acknowledgment rather than dismissal.
Secondary Risk — Iran Deal Unravels Before Friday
The structural caution is real: Iranian and US public statements on Hormuz administration and toll fees diverge publicly. If either side walks back commitments before Friday's ceremony, expect a violent reversal — oil spikes back, USD safe-haven bid returns, and all three setups' original theses reassert quickly. This remains genuinely possible given the analytical base case that Iran's core demands and US red lines are not fully reconciled.
