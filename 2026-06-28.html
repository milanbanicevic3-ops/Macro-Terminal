PCE Confirms the Hawkish Path
Headline 4.1% YoY, Core 3.4% YoY — Both In Line
May PCE printed exactly in line with expectations on both headline (4.1% YoY) and core (3.4% YoY) measures — more than double the Fed's 2% target on both counts. Monthly core PCE at 0.3% also matched expectations. Personal spending (+0.7% vs +0.6% expected) and personal income (+0.7% vs +0.4% expected) both beat, with real personal spending also beating (+0.3% vs +0.2%). The largest spending contributions came from financial services/insurance, healthcare, housing/utilities, and fuel/energy. The analytical read: this data doesn't independently surprise markets, but it removes any argument for the Fed to ease and reinforces the case for an extended pause. Combined with resilient labor and strong income growth, the US economy continues to demonstrate it can absorb higher rates without major strain — directly supporting the case for the Fed's extended hawkish hold.
Australia CPI — Genuinely Mixed Signal
Headline 4.2%→4.0%, Core (Trimmed Mean) 3.4%→3.6%
May headline CPI cooled to 4.0% YoY, below market expectations — but this improvement is substantially explained by a ~12% monthly fall in fuel prices (lower global oil prices plus a government excise cut from April) rather than broad disinflation. Without this factor, headline inflation would have been notably higher. The trimmed-mean core measure — which RBA weights more heavily — actually rose to 3.6%, the highest since September 2024, suggesting underlying price pressure broadened rather than narrowed during May. Housing costs remain the largest single inflation driver (~6.5% annually) with rents and new dwelling costs both elevated on construction cost and supply constraints. Food and beverage prices also showed renewed acceleration, particularly meat, dairy, and hospitality services — categories that typically reflect broader domestic wage and cost pressures rather than pure energy pass-through. The analytical conclusion: short-term AUD weakness is consistent with the cooling headline, but the core acceleration argues RBA will weight the trimmed-mean reading more heavily and maintain vigilance rather than pivot dovish.
Fed Regime — The Explicit Framing Shift
This is worth stating plainly for how future briefs should approach Fed analysis: source commentary draws a direct historical parallel to August of the prior year, when the Fed cut rates into high inflation specifically because they judged the labor market to be weak — a decision that subsequently fueled a major rally in metals. The current regime is explicitly framed as the opposite: with forward guidance eliminated, the market can no longer trade based on what the Fed says it intends to do. Instead, the framework going forward should track how markets react to incoming data, and then how the Fed's actions follow that market reaction — a reactive rather than predictive relationship. This is presented as a deliberate fiscal and monetary discipline shift, and it is the direct explanation offered for why metals have been selling off despite ongoing geopolitical risk premium.
