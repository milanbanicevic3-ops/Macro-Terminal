Regime
A genuinely quiet week by the standards of the last month: the dollar is consolidating without a fresh catalyst, holding losses only against the New Zealand dollar, which continues to draw support from the confirmed RBNZ hike. Wednesday's FOMC Minutes produced no real surprises. A New York Fed official said publicly that he doesn't expect the renewed US-Iran friction to drive a sustained oil rally, and oil has in fact pulled back toward $70 from Friday's spike. Separately, Trump characterized Iran as eager to reach a deal — a familiar talking point given a framework agreement was already reached weeks ago, so it reads as routine rhetoric rather than new information. Fed funds futures now price roughly 70% odds of a September hike, down from 80%, and only about 22% odds of a hike at this month's meeting, down from 30%. Directionally dovish, but nowhere near confirming a faster cut path.
Central bank stances
Fed
Hawkish hold, but with hike odds easing on both the near-term and September horizons per Fed funds futures. Minutes this week were a non-event. Warsh testifies before Congress this week — the next real catalyst for any further repricing.
ECB
Steady at 2.25% deposit rate, data-dependent with upside inflation risk still on the table. Sources remain split on whether the next move is a pause or another hike; no fresh signal this week.
BoJ
Yen strengthened on Asian session news that Japan is considering directing its sovereign pension fund (the world's largest) toward greater domestic asset allocation — more JGB demand would mechanically support the yen. This is unlikely to mark the start of a durable uptrend: the market is more likely to treat any dip as a fresh dollar-buying opportunity than to price a genuinely more hawkish BoJ, and PM Takaichi continues to favor dovish-leaning appointees to the Board.
RBNZ
Confirmed 25bp hike, OCR at 2.50%, as established midweek. Continues to be the one currency where the dollar has actually lost ground this week.
BoE / RBA / SNB / BoC
No fresh signals from any of the four this week. BoC's rate decision on Wednesday is the next real catalyst, landing in the same week as US CPI and PPI — a genuinely high-stakes stretch for USD/CAD specifically.
Geopolitical
US-Iran tensions have cooled from Friday's spike without fully resolving — the underlying framework agreement is intact, and this week's rhetoric reads as noise around an already-negotiated deal rather than a new escalation track. Oil's pullback toward $70 is the cleanest evidence that markets are treating Friday's spike as an isolated event rather than the start of a new regime, consistent with the structural floor/ceiling mechanism flagged Friday (roughly $60–65 floor, $100 ceiling on a genuine re-escalation).

Analyst Conclusion
Dominant theme, primary risk, clearest setups
This was a genuinely quiet week functioning as a coiled spring ahead of what's likely the heaviest calendar stretch since the mid-June FOMC/BoJ resolution week: US CPI, a BoC decision that directly tests the USD/CAD Thesis Invalidation, an ECB decision, Warsh's testimony, and UK CPI and employment all land within five days. The primary risk is straightforward — this is a week to trim size into events rather than defend full positions through the calendar on any active setup. USD/CAD recovers modestly on oil's pullback and a fresh COT extreme that held through Friday's stress test, but faces its own binary event Wednesday. GBP/USD holds at Medium with updated levels and two soft signals worth watching together: real COT short-covering and slightly easing Fed hike odds, neither disqualifying on its own. NZD/USD has been formalized this session, but as a short, deliberately fading the board's own bullish source consensus and Strong H4/D1 momentum on the view that safe-haven flows dominate a hawkish hike that was already largely priced in — a genuine contrarian call, flagged as such, not a quiet reversal of prior analysis. This session also produced the cleanest source-discipline catch yet: one model's entire output was built on a factually wrong picture of who currently runs the Fed, which is as close to an objective, checkable error as this process is likely to catch.
