Regime

Both June CPI and PPI came in cooler than expected this week. Falling energy prices did most of the work, but genuine core softening too — a real signal, not just an energy base effect.

That combination has pulled September hike odds below 60%, down sharply from the more-than-fully-priced level reached earlier in the week on the Iran-driven escalation. The dollar fell to a one-month low alongside Treasury yields.

Thursday's Retail Sales complicated the clean "weak consumer" narrative. Headline retail rose only 0.2%, almost entirely a gasoline-price effect, but core retail sales actually rose a solid 0.5%, helped by Prime Day and World Cup-related spending. The desk's own read: this doesn't justify hikes, but doesn't justify cuts either — the most likely scenario is a long Fed pause. That reframes the whole trade: the dollar itself may not move much from here, but currencies already in a tightening cycle (RBNZ, RBA, BoE) should keep outperforming against it specifically — a real, direct challenge to two of this desk's three active setups, see Top Setups.

Central bank stances
Fed

Warsh's Senate testimony Wednesday was word for word identical to his House remarks Tuesday. No incremental signal either way. He reaffirmed the Committee has "no tolerance for persistently elevated inflation" without committing to a rate path.

A handful of officials, notably Governor Lisa Cook, remain openly willing to hike further. That's a minority view, not a Committee consensus.

ECB
Steady. No fresh signal this week specifically, though the broader "hawkish-tightening-cycle currencies outperform" framework doesn't yet clearly include EUR the way it includes GBP, AUD, and NZD — worth watching for confirmation rather than assuming EUR joins that group automatically.
BoJ

Yen remains the one currency that hasn't benefited from this week's broad dollar softness. Still down on the week even after the earlier GPIF domestic-reallocation headlines.

Same structural reason as every prior session. PM Takaichi's stimulative policy preference and her influence over Board appointments continue to cap any durable yen bid.

BoC

Held at 2.25% as expected. The full statement describes resumed Q2 growth (~2.5% annualized, broadening beyond temporary factors) and elevated near-term inflation (May CPI 3.2%, driven by war-linked gasoline costs, core close to 2%).

The closing language is fully standard and two-sided: the rate "remains appropriate," the Bank "prepared to adjust as needed." Neither an explicit hawkish removal of easing bias nor a dovish signal — closer to neutral-firm than either extreme, and more upbeat on growth than most source characterizations suggested. CAD's depreciation is attributed in the statement itself to a widening yield differential, not a policy signal.

RBNZ / RBA / BoE

Named explicitly in the desk's own updated framework as the currencies that should keep outperforming a pausing dollar. RBNZ and RBA already in active tightening cycles, GBP freshly boosted by fiscal-conservative political news below. This is the core fundamental tension in this week's brief.

Politics & geopolitical
UK politics — genuine GBP driver

Sterling has held most of a sharp rally sparked by Chancellor speculation. Reports that incoming PM Andy Burnham is considering current Home Secretary Shabana Mahmood, seen as fiscally conservative within Labour's moderate wing, reducing perceived risk of a budget that could trigger a 2022 Truss-style crisis.

UK gilts rallied too — the 10-year yield dropped back below 5%. This is real political news with a direct fundamental read-through on GBP, not noise.

Middle East

Conflict continues without signs of cooling. US strikes remain focused on the Iranian coast to degrade Iran's ability to threaten Hormuz shipping; Iran's Revolutionary Guard continues targeting US military bases in the region. Oil holds near $80.

Markets are somewhat reassured on two points. Some tanker traffic still moves through Hormuz, and Iranian retaliation has stayed focused on US targets rather than expanding to Arab-state energy infrastructure, unlike the conflict's earlier peak.

Analyst Conclusion
Dominant theme, primary risk, clearest setups

This week resolved into a genuine Fed long pause rather than a clean directional outcome. Cooler CPI and PPI, a neutral BoC that neither confirmed nor invalidated the USD/CAD thesis, a Warsh testimony that repeated itself word for word, and a Retail Sales print that quietly denied the desk's own pre-set trigger for fresh dollar shorts.

The real story to carry into next week isn't dollar direction at all. This pause reframes the FX map around which currencies are already tightening — genuinely bad news for two of this desk's three active setups. Both GBP/USD and NZD/USD shorts now face a real, structural fundamental headwind from the desk's own updated framework, on top of technical pictures unhelpful for over a week in NZD's case. Both were downgraded honestly this session rather than defended.

USD/CAD stays the least-resolved story of the three. The BoC didn't deliver the clean hawkish confirmation the setup wanted, but it didn't deliver a dovish invalidation either, and the technical picture remains among the strongest in the entire series. Gold's disciplined sell bias enters its fourth consecutive session unchanged, and weekend Iran-US headline risk remains exactly as live as it's been for three straight Fridays.
