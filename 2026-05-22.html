The Week In One Read

The Regime Got Its Institutional Anchor
The week's defining event was the FOMC minutes showing more policymakers open to a rate hike — the first time the institutional Fed voice has aligned with what the CPI, PPI, and Retail Sales prints had already signaled. December hike pricing firmed to 40-50%, terminal-rate expectations rose, and the DXY logged an eighth consecutive session of higher highs. Strong US PMIs (manufacturing 55.3) and firm jobless claims (209k) removed the labor-softening counter-narrative. This is no longer a one-print inflation scare; it is a confirmed, institutionally anchored regime. The dollar is bid on a combination of yield differential, safe-haven flows from the unresolved Iran conflict, and now a Fed whose minutes lean restrictive. The setups that ride USD strength are not fighting the structure — they are riding it.
But The Growth Underneath Is Narrowing
The honest counterweight: the quality of US growth is deteriorating beneath the strong headline data. Consumption growth is increasingly concentrated in energy and gasoline — reflecting higher prices, not greater volume — while discretionary sectors such as restaurants, apparel, and electronics weaken or contract. The consumer is reallocating budget to cover essentials, not spending from surplus. Energy is masking softness across the rest of the economy. The result is the textbook stagflation bind: inflation stays elevated because energy holds the headline up, while real activity slows underneath. Unlike the broad, demand-led growth of the post-pandemic recovery, today's growth is narrow, inflationary, and supply-shock driven — not sustainable. Next week's PCE is the first clean read on whether the crack is widening. If core PCE stays hot while the consumer-facing components soften, the stagflation read strengthens and the USD-bid regime starts resting on a less stable footing.
