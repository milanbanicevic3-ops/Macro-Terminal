Three Things That Matter Into PCE Week's Sequel

The COT Story Is Three Extremes Pulling The Same Direction
The new CFTC release (week ending May 26) printed three positioning extremes that align perfectly with the rotated book. JPY net short hit a fresh cycle extreme at -114,667 — specs added 27,152 contracts even as the BoJ hike pricing firmed and Ueda turned hawkish, making the squeeze setup, when it triggers on June 16-17, larger than at any prior point. NZD short positioning covered to -34,179 from -40,613 — specs rotating bullish into the hawkish RBNZ, validating the Setup 2 long flip. Gold longs normalized to +154,260 — less crowded, healthier setup for the gated re-entry. The fourth notable shift: CAD short doubled to -68,882, specs going max bearish into the Iran-deal narrative. That extreme is a tinderbox — the doubled short provides the fuel for a violent CAD rally if Trump approves the MoU, or partial USD recovery if he stalls.
The MoU Is On The Table, But Trump Hasn't Signed
Thursday brought the genuine breakthrough: a 60-day Memorandum of Understanding agreed between the US and Iran covering full Hormuz transit reopening, the lifting of the US blockade on Iranian ports, and an Iranian commitment not to develop nuclear weapons. Trump was briefed on the final terms and asked for a few days to consider. Markets are pricing the deal as 60-70% likely — the dollar is set for a weekly loss, the December Fed hike probability has dropped from prior to 60%, and oil stabilized in the $90-100 zone. But the weekend introduced specific friction. Trump publicly demanded "unrestricted" Hormuz access, which is incompatible with the Iran-Oman joint-management proposal currently on the table. Bessent signaled "more action possible on Iran if needed." The US blockade remained active, with US Navy turning away a vessel headed for an Iranian port. Iran warned that transit violations would "endanger ships." The deal narrative holds the market for now, but the implementation friction is real, and the resolution this week is the dominant macro variable — bigger than ISM Manufacturing, bigger than ECB Thursday, bigger than NFP Friday.
The Book Is Catalyst-Aligned For The First Time In Weeks
For the prior four weeks the book had been managing through a regime that didn't fully back the trades — the USD/JPY short fought a grinding higher pair, the EUR and AUD shorts rode against eventual risk-on. This week the configuration is different. The three active setups are each catalyst-aligned: USD/JPY short has the BoJ June 16-17 catalyst forming early via Ueda's hawkish commentary plus the cycle-extreme COT short positioning; NZD/USD long has the hawkish RBNZ plus risk-on plus the H1 ADX V.STRONG 61.59 (the strongest reading on the board); XAU/USD long has the soft PCE plus lower oil reducing real yields plus normalized positioning, gated on a clean trigger that hasn't yet fired. The AUD runner is positioned in the unwind that COT is now showing. The setups don't need the Iran deal to work as primary thesis — BoJ does the JPY work, RBNZ does the NZD work, real yields do the gold work — but the deal's resolution either accelerates or stalls all three.

What Worked
The Wednesday risk management was the week's most consequential decision set. Trailing the USD/CAD long stop up to 1.3740 converted the trade from "fighting the Friday selloff" into a logged win — the H4 close below 1.3740 took it out cleanly, locking roughly 30 pips before the broad USD weakness compressed the move. Standing aside on the NZD short Wednesday avoided buying the wrong side of a hawkish-RBNZ rally that subsequently turned into the Friday risk-on extension. Keeping USD/JPY on squeeze-standby refused to feature a direction in the intervention zone for a third straight week — and the standby is now positioned for exactly the move it was built for, with the catalyst converging. Three discipline decisions, three correct outcomes.
What Didn't, And Why It's Not A Failure
The EUR and AUD shorts both scratched into the regime flip without stops hit and without their Thesis Invalidations fully triggering. EUR's TI required "US PCE undershoots materially" — the +0.2% miss versus +0.3% expected was mild, not material, registering only a partial signal. AUD's TI required "China stimulus OR RBA hike" — neither happened. What killed the tactical performance was the Friday risk-on dollar selloff, which is the named risk Sunday's brief flagged as the primary threat to the short complex. The fact that the trades scratched rather than stopped is the framework absorbing the scenario it was warned about. The Thesis Invalidations behaved correctly — they didn't false-trigger, they didn't hold beyond their justification — they were calibrated for the inflation-regime continuation, and a regime tip is a different category of event. This week's setups carry catalyst-aware TIs built for the new regime.
Cumulative Framework Note Through Week 22
Across the prior eight weeks the framework has converted four featured-trade wins (USD/JPY short partial booked, AUD/USD short partial, EUR/USD short partial, USD/CAD long trailed-stop), avoided four featured losses through disciplined standbys and stand-asides, and managed two regime-edge events (the prior JPY squeeze attempt and this week's Iran de-escalation) without portfolio-level damage. The pattern that distinguishes the workable trades from the trapped ones is the alignment of positioning, momentum, and named catalyst — when all three point the same way, the trade works; when only one or two are aligned, the setup needs to be either passive (runner) or gated (XAU). The three actives going into Week 23 (USD/JPY, NZD/USD, XAU/USD) all have the three-way alignment for the first time in months.

Iran: The MoU Is Agreed, Trump Hasn't Signed
Thursday delivered the genuine breakthrough: a 60-day Memorandum of Understanding was agreed between the US and Iran covering full Hormuz transit reopening, the lifting of the US blockade on Iranian ports, and an Iranian commitment not to develop nuclear weapons. Trump was briefed on the final terms and asked for a few days to consider, with the December Fed hike probability dropping to roughly 60% on the deal narrative. The weekend introduced specific friction. Trump publicly demanded "unrestricted" Hormuz access, which is incompatible with the Iran-Oman joint-management proposal currently on the table — Bessent reiterated that "Trump seeks free, open access to Strait of Hormuz." US Navy turned away a vessel headed for an Iranian port; the blockade remained active. Bessent signaled "more action possible on Iran if needed." Iran warned that transit violations would "endanger ships," and the IRGC continues to assert that navy permission and coordination are mandatory for transit. The deal narrative is held by markets but the implementation specifics are contested. Resolution likely lands between Trump's statement (any day this week) and Friday's NFP.
BoJ June 16-17: The Squeeze Catalyst
Ueda's hawkish commentary remains the lead-in. OIS prices a BoJ hike at roughly 70-75% with another nearly fully priced by year-end. Tokyo core inflation slowed to 1.3% YoY (below the 2% target for a fourth consecutive month) but the BoJ pressure persists because consumer prices are expected to re-accelerate from oil and yen weakness. Intervention risk remains high, but for measures to have desired effect the BoJ may need a series of hikes. Otherwise the yen stays under selling pressure. Against the cycle-extreme COT short of -114,667, the BoJ June 16-17 catalyst is what activates Setup 1's full conviction.
ECB June 4: The EUR Direction Resolver
ECB is expected to hike at the June meeting with guidance pointing to additional tightening — but the eurozone is more exposed to the Iran-energy-shock channel than the US, and the composite PMI at 47.5 contraction shows the hawkish stance is hampered by deteriorating growth. Press conference language on growth versus inflation is the EUR/USD swing variable. A hawkish surprise flips the EUR stand-aside to a directional long candidate; a dovish hike or growth-cautious language confirms the stand-aside.
